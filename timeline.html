1066

Death of Prokhore

The founder of the Jvari Monastery (Monastery of the Cross), Prokhore the Georgian, passes away.

1071 - 1078

Seljuk Conquest & First Damage

The Seljuk Turks conquer Ramla and Jerusalem. The monastery likely suffers its first significant damage during this period of intense conflict.

1156

Jruch Synaxarion Created

The Jruch Synaxarion (a liturgical book) is created, indicating continued scholarly and religious life at the monastery.

1177

Ioane Phoka's Visit

The traveler Ioane Phoka visits Jvari and notes its flourishing vineyards. King Baldwin IV of Jerusalem establishes a new annual tax.

1178

Vineyards Confiscated

King Baldwin IV confiscates a portion of Jvari's valuable vineyards for the kingdom's use.

1178 - 1184

Protecting Jvari's Property

Nikoloz Gulaberidze, a prominent Georgian figure, actively works to protect the monastery's remaining property from further encroachment.

1187

Saladin Conquers Jerusalem

Saladin conquers Jerusalem. A Georgian envoy is dispatched to negotiate with him, seeking the return of confiscated holy sites, including Jvari.

1260 - 1277

Jvari Seized by Mamluks

During the rule of Mamluk Sultan Al-Malik Baibars, the Jvari Monastery is seized from the Georgians.

1273

Martyrdom of Luka & Conversion to Mosque

Luka, the Father of Jvari, is martyred. The monastery is officially converted into a mosque, a profound blow to the Georgian community.

1299

Aid from King Vakhtang III

Twenty-six years into its 'captivity,' King Vakhtang III provides crucial aid to the struggling Georgian community at Jvari.

1305

JVARI RETURNED TO GEORGIANS

A TURNING POINT: Through the diplomatic efforts of King David VIII, the Jvari Monastery is officially returned to the Georgians.

1305 - 1314

The "Second Building"

A period known as Jvari's 'second building' begins. The monastery is repaired and its frescoes are renewed, breathing new life into the reclaimed site.

1314

Martyrdom of Nikoloz Dvali

Nikoloz Dvali is martyred in Jerusalem. The powerful narratives of the Passion of Luka and Nikoloz are likely written around this time.

1316 - 1320

Diplomacy with the Sultan

King Giorgi V 'The Brilliant' sends envoys to the Mamluk Sultan in Cairo to secure Georgian rights in the Holy Land.

1331 - 1341

Georgians Hold the Keys

The traveler Ludolf of Sudheim notes that the Georgians are in possession of the keys to the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, a sign of their restored status.

1400

A New Era of Patronage

Timur sacks Damascus. Pimen Matskvereli arrives in Jerusalem, marking the beginning of a renewed period of vigorous patronage from Georgian royalty and nobility.

1476

Golgotha Dome Built

Bagrat, King Alexander's nephew, finances the acquisition of Golgotha and the construction of a new dome, a major architectural contribution.

1501

Pilgrimage of Kaikhosro Atabegi

The nobleman Kaikhosro Atabegi makes a pilgrimage to Jerusalem, continuing the tradition of elite patronage.

1536

Royal Restoration Work

King Leon of Kakheti funds the restoration of the Holy Sepulchre and Golgotha, demonstrating continued royal investment from the Georgian homeland.

1643

Major Fresco Renewal

Nikifore Cholokashvili commissions a complete re-freshening of the monastery's art, including the dome, altar, and walls.

1643 - 1649

Abbot Nikifore Cholokashvili

Nikifore Cholokashvili serves his second term as the abbot of Jvari, overseeing this artistic renaissance.

1757 - 1758

Fading Influence

Timote Gabashvili visits Jerusalem, providing valuable accounts of the Georgian presence just as it was beginning to fade.

1808

Fire Destroys Inscription

A devastating fire destroys the Golgotha Patriarchal Throne inscription, a significant loss of a historical artifact.

1954

Academic Rediscovery Begins

A microfilm of the Tischendorf manuscript is received by the Georgian State Museum, sparking modern academic interest in Jvari's history.

1962

History of the Georgian Colony Published

Elene Metreveli publishes her landmark work, 'Materials for the History of the Georgian Colony in Jerusalem,' cementing the monastery's story in modern scholarship.
